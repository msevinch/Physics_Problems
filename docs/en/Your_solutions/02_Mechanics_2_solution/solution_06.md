### 6. Energy Dissipation


### Fundamental Theory

This problem revolves around **Gravitational Potential Energy (GPE)** and **Energy Dissipation** during inelastic collisions.

1.  **Potential Energy:** When the tennis ball is at its maximum height, its velocity is zero, meaning its kinetic energy is zero. All of its mechanical energy ($E$) is stored as Gravitational Potential Energy. The formula is:
    $$E = PE = mgh$$
    *(where $m$ is mass, $g$ is gravity, and $h$ is height).*

2.  **Energy Loss:** The problem states the ball *loses* 30% of its mechanical energy upon each impact with the ground. This means it *retains* 70% (or 0.70) of its energy after every bounce. Because mass ($m$) and gravity ($g$) remain constant, the maximum height the ball reaches is directly proportional to its remaining total mechanical energy.



---

### Step 1: Define the Initial State

Before the ball is dropped, it is at an initial height ($h_0$) of **2.0 m**.
Its initial mechanical energy ($E_0$) is:
$$E_0 = mgh_0$$

### Step 2: Calculate the State After the First Bounce

When the ball hits the ground the first time, it loses 30% of $E_0$. The energy it has left to carry it back upwards ($E_1$) is 70% of its initial energy:
$$E_1 = 0.70 \cdot E_0$$

Since $E_1 = mgh_1$ and $E_0 = mgh_0$, we can substitute these into our equation:
$$mgh_1 = 0.70 \cdot mgh_0$$

Notice that mass ($m$) and gravity ($g$) appear on both sides of the equation. We can cancel them out entirely, proving that the height decreases by the exact same percentage as the energy:
$$h_1 = 0.70 \cdot h_0$$

Substitute the initial height of 2.0 m to find the height after the first bounce:
$$h_1 = 0.70 \cdot 2.0$$
$$h_1 = 1.4 \text{ meters}$$

### Step 3: Calculate the State After the Second Bounce

The ball falls from its new height of 1.4 m. When it hits the ground for the second time, it loses 30% of its *current* energy ($E_1$). It retains 70% of $E_1$.
$$E_2 = 0.70 \cdot E_1$$

Following the exact same logic as above, the height after the second bounce ($h_2$) will simply be 70% of the height of the first bounce ($h_1$):
$$h_2 = 0.70 \cdot h_1$$

Substitute the known value of $h_1$:
$$h_2 = 0.70 \cdot 1.4$$
$$h_2 = 0.98 \text{ meters}$$

*(Alternative shortcut method: $h_2 = h_0 \cdot (0.70)^2 = 2.0 \cdot 0.49 = 0.98 \text{ m}$)*

**Answer:** After the second bounce, the tennis ball rises to a maximum height of exactly **0.98 meters**.
