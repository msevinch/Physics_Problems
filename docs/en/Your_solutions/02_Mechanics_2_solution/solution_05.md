### 5. Inelastic Collision


### Fundamental Theory

When two objects collide and stick together, moving as a single unit afterward, this is called a **perfectly inelastic collision**. To solve this, we must look at two distinct physical principles:

1.  **Conservation of Linear Momentum:** In any isolated collision (where no external forces like severe friction are acting on the system from the outside), the total momentum before the crash is exactly equal to the total momentum after the crash. 
    $$p_{initial} = p_{final}$$
2.  **Kinetic Energy in Inelastic Collisions:** Unlike momentum, kinetic energy (the energy of macroscopic motion) is **never conserved** in an inelastic collision. Some of the initial kinetic energy is transformed into other forms of energy, such as thermal energy (heat), sound, or the deformation of the objects involved.



---

### Step 1: Determine the Final Speed ($v_f$)

**1. Define the momentum equation:**
Before the jump, only the runner has momentum. After the jump, the runner and the cart combine their masses and move together at a new final velocity ($v_f$).
$$m_{runner}v_{runner} + m_{cart}v_{cart} = (m_{runner} + m_{cart})v_f$$

**2. Substitute the known values:**
* Mass of runner ($m_{runner}$) = **70 kg**
* Initial speed of runner ($v_{runner}$) = **3 m/s**
* Mass of cart ($m_{cart}$) = **140 kg**
* Initial speed of cart ($v_{cart}$) = **0 m/s** (stationary)

$$70(3) + 140(0) = (70 + 140)v_f$$
$$210 + 0 = 210 \cdot v_f$$

**3. Isolate and solve for $v_f$:**
$$210 = 210 \cdot v_f$$
$$v_f = \frac{210}{210}$$
$$v_f = 1 \text{ m/s}$$

**Answer (Part 1):** The final speed of the cart with the runner on it is exactly **1 m/s**.

---

### Step 2: Analyze the Kinetic Energy ($KE$)

To prove whether kinetic energy is conserved, we must calculate the total $KE$ of the system before and after the collision using the formula $KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$.

**1. Calculate Initial Kinetic Energy ($KE_{initial}$):**
Before the jump, only the runner is moving.
$$KE_{initial} = \frac{1}{2}m_{runner}v_{runner}^2$$
$$KE_{initial} = \frac{1}{2}(70)(3)^2$$
$$KE_{initial} = 35 \cdot 9$$
$$KE_{initial} = 315 \text{ Joules}$$

**2. Calculate Final Kinetic Energy ($KE_{final}$):**
After the jump, the combined mass is moving at the new speed we just calculated (1 m/s).
$$KE_{final} = \frac{1}{2}(m_{runner} + m_{cart})v_f^2$$
$$KE_{final} = \frac{1}{2}(70 + 140)(1)^2$$
$$KE_{final} = \frac{1}{2}(210)(1)$$
$$KE_{final} = 105 \text{ Joules}$$

**3. Compare the values:**
$$315 \text{ J} \neq 105 \text{ J}$$

**Answer (Part 2):** **No, kinetic energy is not conserved.** **Explanation:** The system started with 315 Joules of kinetic energy and ended with only 105 Joules. Exactly 210 Joules of energy were "lost" from the macroscopic motion of the system. In reality, this energy didn't vanish; it was converted into other forms. When the runner landed on the cart, their shoes skidded against the surface (creating heat via friction), their muscles absorbed the impact (internal biomechanical energy), and the collision likely made a noise (sound energy).
