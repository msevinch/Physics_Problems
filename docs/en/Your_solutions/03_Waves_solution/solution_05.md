
This problem introduces a classic, real-world application of wave reflection known as echo ranging (which is the exact same fundamental principle used by sonar and radar).

### The Underlying Theory: The "Round Trip"

When you shout toward a cliff, the sound wave does not just travel to the cliff; it must also bounce off the rock face and travel all the way back to your ears for you to hear the echo. 

This means the time you measure (1 second) is the time for a **round trip**. The sound wave travels the distance between you and the cliff *twice*. 

Therefore, the formula relating distance ($d$), velocity ($v$), and time ($t$) for an echo is:
$$d = \frac{v \times t}{2}$$

Where:
* **$d$** is the one-way distance to the reflecting surface (the cliff).
* **$v$** is the speed of the wave.
* **$t$** is the total time from the creation of the sound to the return of the echo.
* We divide by **2** because we only want the distance *to* the cliff, not the distance to the cliff and back.

---

### Step 1: Identify the Known Variables

From your problem, we have:
* Speed of sound in air ($v$) = **343 m/s**
* Total round-trip time ($t$) = **1 s**

---

### Step 2: Calculate the Total Distance Traveled by the Sound

First, let's find out exactly how far the sound wave traveled in total during that 1 second. We use the basic distance formula ($Distance = Velocity \times Time$):
$$d_{total} = v \times t$$
$$d_{total} = 343 \text{ m/s} \times 1 \text{ s}$$
$$d_{total} = 343 \text{ meters}$$

During that 1 second, the sound wave physically traveled a total of 343 meters through the air.

---

### Step 3: Calculate the Distance to the Cliff

Because that 343 meters represents the journey *to* the cliff and back to you, the actual distance to the cliff is exactly half of that total distance:
$$d = \frac{343 \text{ m}}{2}$$
$$d = 171.5 \text{ meters}$$

### Final Answer

The cliff is exactly **171.5 meters** away from the person.
