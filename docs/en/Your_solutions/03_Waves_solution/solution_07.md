
### The Underlying Theory: Standing Wave Modes

When a string is fixed at both ends (like a guitar or piano string), any standing wave that forms on it must have zero displacement at those fixed ends. These points of zero movement are called **nodes**. 

The points of maximum vibration between the nodes are called **antinodes**. Each visible "loop" or "football" shape on a vibrating string corresponds to exactly one antinode.

A fundamental rule of wave mechanics is that **one single loop (one antinode) represents exactly half of a wavelength**. 
Therefore, the total length of the string ($L$) is simply the number of loops ($n$) multiplied by half a wavelength:
$$L = n \times \frac{\lambda}{2}$$

Where:
* **$L$** is the total length of the string.
* **$n$** is the number of antinodes (which also represents the *harmonic number*).
* **$\lambda$** (lambda) is the full wavelength.

---

### Step 1: Identify the Wave Pattern

From your problem description, we know two things:
1.  The string has a total length of **80 cm** (which is 0.8 meters).
2.  The wave produces exactly **four antinodes**.

Because there are four antinodes, the string is vibrating in its 4th harmonic ($n = 4$). This means the string is divided into four equal vibrating loops.

---

### Step 2: Set Up the Equation

Now we plug our known values into the standing wave equation:
* $L = 0.8 \text{ m}$
* $n = 4$

$$0.8 \text{ m} = 4 \times \frac{\lambda}{2}$$

---

### Step 3: Solve for Wavelength ($\lambda$)

First, simplify the right side of the equation by dividing 4 by 2:
$$0.8 \text{ m} = 2\lambda$$

This mathematically confirms what we can visualize: if four half-wavelengths fit on the string, that means exactly two full wavelengths fit on the string. 

Now, divide both sides by 2 to isolate the wavelength ($\lambda$):
$$\lambda = \frac{0.8 \text{ m}}{2}$$
$$\lambda = 0.4 \text{ meters}$$

*(Or 40 cm, if you prefer to keep it in the original units).*

### Final Answer
The wavelength of the standing wave is **0.4 meters** (or **40 cm**).
