
### The Underlying Theory: Standing Waves
When you pluck a guitar string, the wave travels down the string, reflects off the fixed bridge at the other end, and travels back. These continuous reflections interfere with one another to create what physicists call a **standing wave**. 

Because the string is held tight (fixed) at both ends, those end points cannot move. They are called **nodes**. 
The prompt mentions that the string vibrates at its fundamental frequency with exactly one **antinode**. An antinode is the point of maximum vibration. A pattern with a node at each end and exactly one antinode in the middle forms a single "loop" or "football" shape. 

In wave mechanics, a full wavelength ($\lambda$) consists of *two* such loops (one "up" peak and one "down" trough). Therefore, a string vibrating with only one loop is holding exactly **half of a wavelength**. 

Mathematically, the relationship between the length of the string ($L$) and the wavelength ($\lambda$) for the fundamental frequency is:
$$L = \frac{\lambda}{2}$$

Which means the full wavelength is twice the length of the string:
$$\lambda = 2L$$

---

### Step 1: Find the Wavelength ($\lambda$)

First, we need to convert the length of the string into standard SI units (meters) so our final speed will be in meters per second (m/s).
* Length ($L$) = **64 cm** = **0.64 meters**

Now, we use our formula to find the wavelength of the fundamental frequency:
$$\lambda = 2 \times 0.64 \text{ m}$$
$$\lambda = 1.28 \text{ meters}$$

This means that if the wave weren't trapped on the 0.64-meter string, a single full wave cycle would stretch out to 1.28 meters.

---

### Step 2: Calculate the Wave Speed ($v$)

Now that we have the wavelength, we can use the universal wave equation, which relates speed ($v$), frequency ($f$), and wavelength ($\lambda$):
$$v = f \times \lambda$$

We plug in our known values:
* Frequency ($f$) = **330 Hz** (which means 330 cycles per second)
* Wavelength ($\lambda$) = **1.28 meters**

$$v = 330 \text{ Hz} \times 1.28 \text{ m}$$
$$v = 422.4 \text{ m/s}$$

### Final Answer
The speed of the wave traveling along the guitar string is **422.4 m/s**.
