

### The Underlying Theory
To solve this, we rely on the universal wave equation, which defines the relationship between a wave's speed ($v$), its frequency ($f$), and its wavelength ($\lambda$). 

The equation is:
$$v = f \times \lambda$$

Where:
* **$v$** is the velocity (speed) of the wave, measured in meters per second (m/s).
* **$f$** is the frequency, measured in Hertz (Hz), which represents the number of wave cycles per second.
* **$\lambda$** (lambda) is the wavelength, measured in meters (m), which is the physical distance between two consecutive peaks of the wave.

**A Crucial Concept:** When a wave travels from one medium (like air) into another medium (like water), its speed and wavelength change, but **its frequency remains completely constant**. The frequency is entirely determined by the original source vibrating to create the sound (in this case, producing 440 vibrations per second, which corresponds to the musical note A4). Therefore, we will use $f = 440 \text{ Hz}$ for both parts of the problem.

---

### Step 1: Finding the Wavelength in Air

First, we isolate wavelength ($\lambda$) in our formula:
$$\lambda = \frac{v}{f}$$

Now, we plug in the given values for air:
* $v_{air} = 343 \text{ m/s}$
* $f = 440 \text{ Hz}$

$$\lambda_{air} = \frac{343 \text{ m/s}}{440 \text{ Hz}}$$
$$\lambda_{air} = 0.77954... \text{ meters}$$

**Answer for Air:** The wavelength of the 440 Hz sound wave in air is approximately **0.78 meters** (or 78 centimeters).

---

### Step 2: Finding the Wavelength in Water

We use the exact same formula, keeping the frequency the same, but substituting the new speed of sound in water. Sound travels much faster in water because liquid is denser and less compressible than a gas, allowing mechanical waves to transfer energy more rapidly from particle to particle.

* $v_{water} = 1482 \text{ m/s}$
* $f = 440 \text{ Hz}$

$$\lambda_{water} = \frac{v_{water}}{f}$$
$$\lambda_{water} = \frac{1482 \text{ m/s}}{440 \text{ Hz}}$$
$$\lambda_{water} = 3.36818... \text{ meters}$$

**Answer for Water:** The wavelength of the 440 Hz sound wave in water is approximately **3.37 meters**.

### Summary
Because the wave travels much faster in water while maintaining the exact same rate of vibration (440 cycles per second), the wave stretches out, resulting in a much longer wavelength in the water (3.37 m) compared to the air (0.78 m).
