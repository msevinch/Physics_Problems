
### The Underlying Theory: Mapping Space to Angles

To understand phase difference, we have to look at how a wave maps onto a circle. 
A wave is a repeating, cyclical pattern. If you pick a specific point on a wave, it will go through a full cycle (up to a peak, down to a trough, and back to the starting position) over a physical distance of exactly one wavelength ($\lambda$).

In physics and mathematics, we often describe these cycles using angles—specifically, **radians**. 
* One complete cycle of a circle is **$2\pi$ radians**.
* Therefore, traveling a physical distance of one full wavelength ($\lambda$) along the wave corresponds to moving through a phase angle of **$2\pi$ radians**.

Because the wave is uniform, the relationship between physical distance (path difference, $\Delta x$) and angular distance (phase difference, $\Delta\phi$) is perfectly proportional.

---

### Step 1: Setting Up the Formula

We can set up a simple ratio based on the proportional relationship described above:
$$\frac{\text{Phase Difference}}{\text{Full Cycle in Radians}} = \frac{\text{Path Difference}}{\text{Full Wavelength}}$$

Mathematically, this is written as:
$$\frac{\Delta\phi}{2\pi} = \frac{\Delta x}{\lambda}$$

To solve for the phase difference ($\Delta\phi$), we rearrange the equation:
$$\Delta\phi = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} \times \Delta x$$

*(Note: The term $\frac{2\pi}{\lambda}$ is also known as the **wave number**, often represented by the letter $k$.)*

---

### Step 2: Plugging in the Given Values

The problem states that the two points on the wave are separated by a physical distance of one-third of a wavelength.
* Path Difference ($\Delta x$) = $\frac{\lambda}{3}$

Now, we substitute this into our rearranged formula:
$$\Delta\phi = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} \times \left(\frac{\lambda}{3}\right)$$

---

### Step 3: Solving the Equation

Notice that we have wavelength ($\lambda$) in both the numerator and the denominator. They neatly cancel each other out:
$$\Delta\phi = 2\pi \times \left(\frac{1}{3}\right)$$
$$\Delta\phi = \frac{2\pi}{3}$$

### Final Answer

The phase difference between two points separated by a distance of $\lambda/3$ is **$\frac{2\pi}{3}$ radians** (which is equivalent to 120 degrees).
