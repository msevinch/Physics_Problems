Here is the step-by-step solution for calculating the percentage uncertainty of your time measurement.

### **1. Theory: Percentage Uncertainty**

When you record a measurement, the **absolute uncertainty** (often denoted as $\delta t$) represents the physical margin of error in the same units as the measurement itself. In your case, this is the $0.22$ seconds.

To understand how significant that error is compared to the total measurement, we calculate the **relative uncertainty** (or fractional uncertainty), which is the ratio of the absolute uncertainty to the measured value ($t$).

When you multiply this ratio by 100, it becomes the **percentage uncertainty**. This gives you a clear picture of the error's scale regardless of the units used.

The formula is:


$$\text{Percentage Uncertainty} = \left( \frac{\delta t}{t} \right) \times 100$$

---

### **2. Determination: Step-by-Step Calculation**

**Given Values:**

* Measured time ($t$) = **5.45 seconds**
* Absolute uncertainty ($\delta t$) = **0.22 seconds**

#### **Step A: Calculate the Relative Uncertainty**

Divide the absolute uncertainty by the measured value:


$$\text{Relative Uncertainty} = \frac{0.22}{5.45}$$

$$\text{Relative Uncertainty} \approx 0.040367$$

#### **Step B: Convert to a Percentage**

Multiply the relative uncertainty by 100 to find the percentage:


$$\text{Percentage Uncertainty} = 0.040367 \times 100$$

$$\text{Percentage Uncertainty} \approx 4.0367\%$$

#### **Step C: Rounding and Significant Figures**

Experimental uncertainties are typically rounded to one or two significant figures. Since your absolute uncertainty (**0.22**) has two significant figures, it is standard practice to report the percentage uncertainty to two significant figures as well.

* Rounding **4.0367%** to two significant figures gives us **4.0%**.

---

### **Final Answer**

The percentage uncertainty of the time measurement is **4.0%**.
