
### **1. Theory: Instrument Resolution and Precision**

In experimental science, the smallest interval an instrument can detect is called its **resolution**. For a digital instrument, the resolution is always the value of the last displayed digit (the Least Significant Digit, or LSD).

There are two common conventions for assigning reading uncertainty to an instrument:

1. **The Full-Digit Convention:** Assigning the entire value of the last digit as the uncertainty (common for digital displays because they round or truncate numbers).
2. **The Half-Digit Convention:** Assigning half the value of the last digit as the uncertainty (traditionally used for analog scales where a human eye can estimate halfway between lines, but sometimes specified for digital instruments too).

Since this problem explicitly instructs us to use the **half-digit convention**, our theoretical formula for the absolute uncertainty ($\delta T$) is:


$$\delta T = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{Value of the Last Digit}$$

### **2. Determination: Step-by-Step Calculation**

**Given Value:**

* Thermometer reading = 25.4°C

#### **Step A: Identify the Value of the Last Digit**

Look at the digital reading: 25.**4**°C.
The last digit is in the tenths place. Therefore, the smallest unit this thermometer can resolve—the value of its last digit—is **0.1°C**.

#### **Step B: Calculate the Absolute Uncertainty ($\delta T$)**

Using the rule provided, divide the value of this last digit by 2:


$$\delta T = \frac{0.1^\circ\text{C}}{2}$$

$$\delta T = 0.05^\circ\text{C}$$

### **Final Answer**

The absolute uncertainty of this measurement is **±0.05°C**.
