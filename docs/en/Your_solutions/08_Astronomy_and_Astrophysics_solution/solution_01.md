### **The Theory: Rotational Motion**

When an object rotates around a fixed axis, every point on that object travels in a circle. The **linear speed** ($v$) of a point on a rotating sphere depends on two things: how far it is from the axis of rotation (the radius, $R$) and how long it takes to complete one full spin (the period, $T$).

The distance covered in one full rotation is simply the circumference of the circle at that point. For a point on the equator, this circle has a radius equal to the Earth's equatorial radius. The formula for circumference ($C$) is:


$$C = 2\pi R$$

Since speed is defined as distance divided by time, the linear rotational speed ($v$) is given by the formula:


$$v = \frac{2\pi R}{T}$$

Where:

* $v$ = linear speed (in km/s)
* $R$ = radius of the Earth at the equator (in km)
* $T$ = time period for one full rotation (in seconds)

---

### **Determine: Step-by-Step Calculation**

To find the exact speed, we need to establish our known values and plug them into our theoretical formula.

#### **Step 1: Identify the Given Values**

* **Radius ($R$):** $6378\text{ km}$
* **Time Period ($T$):** The Earth takes 24 hours to complete one solar rotation.

#### **Step 2: Convert Time to Seconds**

Because we want the final speed in kilometers per *second*, we must convert the 24-hour rotation period into seconds.

* $1\text{ hour} = 60\text{ minutes}$
* $1\text{ minute} = 60\text{ seconds}$

$$T = 24\text{ hours} \cdot 60\text{ min/hour} \cdot 60\text{ sec/min}$$

$$T = 86,400\text{ seconds}$$

> *Note: For absolute astronomical precision, scientists use a "sidereal day" (the time it takes Earth to rotate relative to distant stars), which is roughly 23 hours, 56 minutes, and 4 seconds (86,164 seconds). We will use the standard 24-hour day (86,400 seconds) as typically required in physics problems.*

#### **Step 3: Calculate the Equatorial Circumference**

Now, determine the total distance a point on the equator travels in one day using $2\pi R$:


$$C = 2 \cdot \pi \cdot 6378\text{ km}$$

$$C \approx 2 \cdot 3.14159 \cdot 6378\text{ km}$$

$$C \approx 40,074.16\text{ km}$$

#### **Step 4: Calculate the Linear Speed ($v$)**

Finally, divide the total distance by the total time to get the speed in km/s:


$$v = \frac{40,074.16\text{ km}}{86,400\text{ s}}$$

$$v \approx 0.46382\text{ km/s}$$

---

### **Final Answer**

The linear speed of a point on the Earth's equator due to its rotation is approximately **0.464 km/s** (or about 1,670 km/h).
