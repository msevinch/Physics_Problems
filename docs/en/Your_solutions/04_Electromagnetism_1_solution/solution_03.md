
---

### 1. Identify the Given Parameters
* **Charge 1 ($q_1$):** $+4\text{ C}$ (placed at $x = 0$)
* **Charge 2 ($q_2$):** $+9\text{ C}$ (placed at $x = 2.0\text{ m}$)
* **Test Charge ($q_3$):** $+1\text{ C}$
* **Total Distance ($L$):** $2.0\text{ m}$

### 2. Define the Geometry
Let $x$ be the distance from $q_1$ to the equilibrium position where $q_3$ is placed. 
* Distance from $q_1$ to $q_3 = x$
* Distance from $q_2$ to $q_3 = 2.0 - x$



### 3. Establish the Theory (Coulomb's Law)
For $q_3$ to be in equilibrium, the forces must balance:
$$\vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \vec{F}_{13} + \vec{F}_{23} = 0$$

Since $q_1$ and $q_2$ are both positive, they both repel $q_3$. For the forces to point in opposite directions, $q_3$ **must** be placed between them. We set the magnitudes of the forces equal to each other:
$$\frac{k |q_1 q_3|}{x^2} = \frac{k |q_2 q_3|}{(2.0 - x)^2}$$

### 4. Step-by-Step Algebraic Solution
**Step A: Simplify the equation**
Notice that Coulomb's constant ($k$) and the value of the test charge ($q_3$) appear on both sides. We can cancel them out:
$$\frac{q_1}{x^2} = \frac{q_2}{(2.0 - x)^2}$$

**Step B: Plug in the charge values**
$$\frac{4}{x^2} = \frac{9}{(2.0 - x)^2}$$

**Step C: Take the square root of both sides**
This is a much cleaner method than expanding into a quadratic equation:
$$\sqrt{\frac{4}{x^2}} = \sqrt{\frac{9}{(2.0 - x)^2}}$$
$$\frac{2}{x} = \frac{3}{2.0 - x}$$

**Step D: Solve for $x$ (Cross-multiplication)**
$$2(2.0 - x) = 3x$$
$$4.0 - 2x = 3x$$
$$4.0 = 5x$$
$$x = \frac{4.0}{5} = 0.8\text{ m}$$

---

### Final Result
The equilibrium position for $q_3$ is **$0.8\text{ m}$ from the $+4\text{ C}$ charge** (which is $1.2\text{ m}$ from the $+9\text{ C}$ charge).

> **Verification:** It makes sense that the equilibrium point is closer to the smaller charge ($q_1 = 4\text{ C}$) than the larger charge ($q_2 = 9\text{ C}$), as the smaller distance compensates for the smaller charge magnitude to maintain a balanced force.
