
First, let's list out our given variables:

* **Primary turns** ($N_p$) = 1000
* **Secondary turns** ($N_s$) = 200
* **Primary voltage** ($V_p$) = 120 V
* **Secondary current** ($I_s$) = 3 A

Since the secondary coil has fewer turns than the primary coil, this is a **step-down transformer**. This means the voltage will decrease, but the current will increase to conserve power.

---

### **Part 1: Finding the Secondary Voltage**

**Theory**
The fundamental principle of an ideal transformer states that the ratio of the voltages across the coils is directly proportional to the ratio of the number of turns in the coils. This is known as the transformer equation:


$$\frac{V_s}{V_p}=\frac{N_s}{N_p}$$

**Determine**
We can rearrange this formula to solve for the secondary voltage ($V_s$):


$$V_s=V_p\left(\frac{N_s}{N_p}\right)$$

Now, plug in the given values:


$$V_s=120\left(\frac{200}{1000}\right)$$

$$V_s=120(0.2)$$

$$V_s=24$$

The secondary voltage is **24 V**.

---

### **Part 2: Finding the Primary Current**

**Theory**
Assuming an ideal transformer with 100% efficiency, the power put into the primary coil equals the power coming out of the secondary coil ($P_{in} = P_{out}$). Because electrical power is voltage multiplied by current ($P = VI$), the relationship between current and the number of turns is *inversely* proportional.


$$\frac{I_p}{I_s}=\frac{N_s}{N_p}$$

**Determine**
We can rearrange this formula to solve for the primary current ($I_p$):


$$I_p=I_s\left(\frac{N_s}{N_p}\right)$$

Now, plug in the given values:


$$I_p=3\left(\frac{200}{1000}\right)$$

$$I_p=3(0.2)$$

$$I_p=0.6$$

The current in the primary coil is **0.6 A**.
