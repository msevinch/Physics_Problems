
### 8. Circular Motion

### Fundamental Theory

Even when you are standing completely still on the equator, you are actually traveling in a massive circle because the Earth is rotating on its axis. According to Newton's First Law, an object in motion wants to travel in a straight line. To keep you moving in a circle instead of flying off into space, there must be a continuous inward force pulling you toward the center of the Earth. 

The acceleration associated with this inward force is called **Centripetal Acceleration** ($a_c$). 



We can calculate this using either your linear speed ($v$) along the edge of the circle or your angular speed ($\omega$). We will use the linear velocity method, which relies on the formula:
$$a_c = \frac{v^2}{r}$$
*(where $v$ is linear velocity and $r$ is the radius of the circular path).*

---

### Step 1: Define the Known Variables and Convert to SI Units

To end up with standard physics units for acceleration (meters per second squared, or m/s²), we must first convert all our given values into meters and seconds.

* **Radius of the Earth ($r$):** We are given 6378 km. 
    $$r = 6378 \text{ km} \times 1000 \text{ m/km} = 6,378,000 \text{ meters}$$
* **Time of one full rotation ($T$):** The Earth completes one full rotation in exactly one day (we will use the standard 24-hour day for this calculation). Let's convert this to seconds:
    $$T = 24 \text{ hours} \times 60 \text{ minutes/hour} \times 60 \text{ seconds/minute}$$
    $$T = 86,400 \text{ seconds}$$

---

### Step 2: Calculate the Linear Velocity ($v$)

Velocity is simply distance divided by time. In one day, a person on the equator travels exactly one circumference of the Earth. The formula for the circumference of a circle is $2\pi r$.

$$v = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Time}} = \frac{2\pi r}{T}$$

Now, plug in our standard SI values:
$$v = \frac{2 \cdot \pi \cdot 6,378,000}{86,400}$$
$$v \approx \frac{40,074,155}{86,400}$$
$$v \approx 463.8 \text{ m/s}$$

*(Fun fact: This means just by standing on the equator, you are hurtling through space at about 464 meters per second, or roughly 1,000 mph!)*

---

### Step 3: Calculate the Centripetal Acceleration ($a_c$)

Now that we have both the velocity and the radius in standard units, we can plug them into the centripetal acceleration formula:

$$a_c = \frac{v^2}{r}$$
$$a_c = \frac{(463.8)^2}{6,378,000}$$
$$a_c = \frac{215,110.44}{6,378,000}$$
$$a_c \approx 0.0337 \text{ m/s}^2$$

**Answer:** The centripetal acceleration of a person standing on the Earth's equator is approximately **0.0337 m/s²**. 

*Context:* This value is very small compared to the Earth's overall gravitational pull (which is about **9.8 m/s²**). This is why you don't feel like you are being flung off the planet; gravity is vastly stronger than the centripetal acceleration required to keep you in circular motion.
